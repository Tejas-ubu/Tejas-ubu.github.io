02 —

Five archetypes of the AI-aware mind

Beneath the data lies something more revealing — how people construct their moral relationship with technology. We clustered respondents not by demographics, but by the internal logic connecting their answers.

Each archetype represents a coherent worldview: a particular balance of trust, skepticism, responsibility, and agency. These aren't labels — they're thought patterns.

How we built these

Responses across 13 questions were mapped onto 5 ethical dimensions. Clusters emerged from co-occurring answer patterns — respondents whose views aligned across trust, accountability, and concern dimensions.

What "archetype" means

Not a label imposed from outside, but a coherent internal logic. Each archetype has a characteristic way of framing risk, assigning responsibility, and thinking about AI's future.

The 5 dimensions

Trust · Verification Rigor · Corporate Accountability · Environmental Priority · Transparency Demand. Each respondent scores across all five; archetypes are peaks in this space.

Trust
How much do they take AI output at face value? Credulous, skeptical, or deeply mistrustful?
Verification Rigor
How systematically do they cross-check AI claims? Always, sometimes, or never?
Corp. Accountability
Who's responsible for AI harms — the company, the user, or a shared governance model?
Env. Priority
Does the carbon cost of AI weigh on them? Would they accept worse models for a greener footprint?
Transparency Demand
How strongly do they want AI labeled, cited, and explained to the public?
Axis of Concern
Is their primary worry about bias, misinformation, IP theft, deepfakes, or environmental harm?

VP
The Vigilant Pragmatist
Scenario: Using an AI tool to research a topic for work or study — then deciding how much to trust and act on the output.
🗣Says
"AI shouldn't replace human critical thinking — use it as a helper."
"There should be strict rules against deepfakes that ruin reputations."
"AI content should always be clearly labeled — no exceptions."
"You need to use AI — don't let AI use you."
🧠Thinks
Can I actually trust this answer, or is it hallucinating?
What happens when someone less critical sees this same output?
Someone needs to be legally responsible when AI causes real harm.
If I don't verify this, I'm part of the problem.
🏃Does
Uses AI daily for study and work tasks
Always or often cross-checks AI output against trusted sources
Advocates vocally for AI labeling and corporate accountability
Notices and calls out AI-generated content in the wild
😌Feels
Cautiously optimistic — AI is useful but risky
Frustrated when others accept AI output uncritically
Responsible — like a citizen with a duty to push back on bad AI
Satisfied after successfully verifying and using AI output well

Key Insights

Highest daily usage combined with highest verification rate — AI is essential but never trusted blindly
Strongly corporate-accountability focused; views companies as the primary responsible party
Labeling demand is near-absolute — transparency is a non-negotiable baseline

Says vs. Does Contradiction

Says AI must be regulated and labeled — but still uses it daily and heavily, suggesting convenience overrides stated caution in practice
Advocates for others to verify AI, yet their own verification is sometimes cursory rather than rigorous

The Cautious Analyst
Scenario: Using AI to look up factual information, then deciding whether the answer is reliable enough to share or act on.
🗣Says
"AI shouldn't be a black box — companies must explain their reasoning."
"There should be a standard way to credit the authors AI was trained on."
"AI needs to prove where it's getting its info from."
"Responsibility should be shared — not just companies, not just users."
🧠Thinks
Where did this information actually come from? Show me the sources.
This might sound right but could be completely fabricated.
The artists and writers whose work trained this model deserve credit.
Misinformation at scale is the most dangerous thing AI could do.
🏃Does
Uses AI daily or weekly but never accepts output without checking
Verifies against books, Wikipedia, official institutional sources
Actively looks for original sources behind AI-surfaced claims
Raises IP and attribution concerns in discussions about AI
😌Feels
Deeply skeptical — low baseline trust in AI output
Protective — wants to defend creators whose work was harvested
Anxious about the misinformation implications at social scale
Mildly hopeful if strong citation standards were ever enforced

Key Insights

Lowest trust score of all archetypes — but still a frequent user, revealing pragmatic necessity over ideological purity
IP and attribution is this persona's defining concern — unique among all five types
Prefers shared accountability model — nuanced, not black-and-white

Says vs. Does Contradiction

Argues AI should cite sources and compensate creators — but still uses AI tools that do neither
Claims misinformation is AI's biggest risk, yet verifies primarily via other digital sources which can themselves contain errors

🧠Thinks

Bias isn't a bug — it reflects the values of whoever built this.

No single company can govern itself — we need enforceable global standards.
🏃Does
Uses AI daily but constantly questions its social implications
Often brings up bias and access in conversations about AI
Supports calls for international AI regulation and human oversight
Very concerned about environmental impact; supports green AI policies
😌Feels

Key Insights

Highest corporate accountability score — wants full responsibility on companies, not shared or user-led
Unique in framing AI ethics as a civil rights and equity issue, not just a safety or accuracy problem

Says vs. Does Contradiction

Argues passionately against data extraction without consent — but uses AI tools daily that operate exactly on that model
Champions universal access but relies on AI products that require expensive subscriptions

Says vs. Does Contradiction

Says "there's not much end users can do" — but that framing itself is a choice not to try
Says privacy matters — but shares personal data with AI systems without reading how it's stored or used
LT
The Libertarian Techno-Optimist
Scenario: Hearing about new proposed AI regulations and forming an opinion on whether they should be supported or resisted.
🗣Says
"We shouldn't over-regulate AI — it will make it useless for the average person."
"Companies shouldn't over-censor AI just to be 'safe' — it kills creativity."
"The biggest rule should just be protecting basic user privacy."
"Users are smart enough to judge AI outputs themselves."
🧠Thinks
Every new regulation is a step toward unusable, watered-down AI.
Privacy is the one real issue — everything else is performative.
AI's power comes from its freedom — restrict it and you break it.
The market will correct for bad AI faster than any government body.
🏃Does
Heaviest daily AI user of all five archetypes — fully integrated
Rarely verifies output — high trust in AI relative to other groups
Pushes back on AI ethics discourse in conversations
Uses AI for creative, professional, and exploratory tasks freely
😌Feels
Excited and empowered — AI is a force multiplier for capable people
Impatient with what they see as moral panic around AI risks
Dismissive of environmental arguments — sees them as scope-creep
Confident in their own judgment — doesn't need guardrails

Key Insights

Highest trust + lowest verification = most exposed to AI error, yet feels least vulnerable — overconfidence is a defining trait
Privacy is the only legitimate concern in their framework — all other ethical concerns dismissed as overreach
Smallest group (~7%) but likely overrepresented in online AI discourse relative to real-world prevalence

Says vs. Does Contradiction

Argues users should take responsibility for verifying AI — but personally verifies the least of any archetype
Advocates for privacy protection as the only real rule — but uses AI tools that routinely harvest personal data without scrutiny
